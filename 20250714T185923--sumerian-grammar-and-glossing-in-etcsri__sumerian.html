"The mixed logographic-phonographic writing system reflects the morphophonological structure of Sumerian to varying extent in different periods. The morpho-phonological structure of Sumerian words must therefore be reconstructed by setting up correspondences between the sequence of graphemes and the sequence of morphemes. This interpretation is necessarily subjective to some extent, and reflects the grammatical model of the interpreter." (Gábor Zólyomi, 2017 page 21)

All models are wrong, but some are useful. We will describe the model used by The Electronic Text Corpus of Sumerian Royal Inscriptions (ETCSRI) (Zólyomi, Gábor - Tanos, Bálint - Sövegjártó, Szilvia, 2008-), because that's the most extensive morphologically analyzed Sumerian corpus to date. ETCSRI uses the model of template morphology.

1. Template morphology   fc

Template morphology descibes words in terms of position classes (Gregory Stump, 2005). A position class is a class of affixes with two properties:

  • in the paradigmatic dimension, affixes in the same class are mutually exclusive, at most one affix of each class can occur in a word;
  • in the syntagmatic dimension, the affixes in a given class always appear in the same order relative to the affixes in every other class (before or after);

Some affix classes may span two or more prefix positions – that is, their members are paradigmatically opposed to any combination of affixes occupying those positions.

3. Noun phrase

3.1. N1, Head

may be occupied by:

  • simple nouns,
  • compound nouns,
  • pronouns,
  • nonfinite verbal forms,
  • clauses with subordinate finite verbal forms.

3.2. N2, Modifier

A noun phrase may have more than one modifiers. may be occupied by:

  • non-finite verbal forms,
  • relative clauses,
  • noun phrases in the genitive case,
  • noun phrases in the ablative case,
  • cardinal numbers,
  • demonstrative pronouns:
morpheme gloss description
e DEM demonstrative pronoun
nee DEM3 demonstrative pronoun

4. Non-finite verb

Remark1: A non-finite verbal form may occupy either N1 or N2. In the first case it functions as a Noun, while in the second case it functions as a modifier. So called adjectives are considered non-finite verbal forms. Remark2: Just like non-finite verbal forms, cardinal numbers may be used both as modifiers and nouns, they will thus be inserted in the non-finite verbal template.

5.12. V12, stem

morhpheme gloss description
CF STEM verbal stem
CF STEM-PF present-future verbal stem
CF STEM-PL plural verbal stem
CF STEM-RDP reduplicated verbal stem
CF COP the independent copula

Remark1: These can be combined, so for example sub2 is STEM-PF-PL, i.e. present-future plural stem of go. The order of qualifying glosses should follow this table (so sub2 may only be glossed as before but not as STEM-PL-PF). Remark2: Unlike in The Penn Parsed Corpus of Sumerian, ETCSRI will not have a special template with special slots for imperative verbal forms: in imperative verbal forms the verbal stem is assumed to occupy V1.

enden 1-PL-A first person plural agent suffix

enzen 2-PL-A second person plural agent suffix

enzen 2-PL second person plural suffix in plural transitive preterite verbal forms
eš 3-PL-S second person plural subject suffix
eš 3-PL-P second person plural patient suffix (df)
eš 3-PL third person plural suffix in plural transitive preterite verbal forms (df)
enee 3-PL-A third person plural agent suffix in present-future verbal forms

Remark1: In transitive preterite forms the plural agents are cross-referenced with a periphrastic construction involving the use of two affixes: the singular form of an FPP in V11 and the plural form of a pronominal suffix in V14, that is the FPP in V11 agrees in gender and person, while the suffix in V14 agrees in number and person with the agent. In these forms the suffix in V14 will be glossed as 1-PL, 2-PL, or 3-PL, i.e. without specifying its syntactic function, e.g.: …-3-SG-H-A-stem-3-PL
